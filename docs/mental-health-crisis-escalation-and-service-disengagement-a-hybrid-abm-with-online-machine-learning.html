Chapter 24 Mental Health Crisis Escalation and Service Disengagement: A Hybrid ABM with Online Machine Learning

The previous model focused on substance use initiation across a fixed socioeconomic hierarchy, where agents adapted their behavior through Q-learning at every organizational level. This model takes a different problem structure. Service disengagement in mental health care is not primarily a learning problem — it is a prediction and allocation problem. Agents drop out of care for reasons that are observable but heterogeneous, and the system’s limited outreach capacity means that guessing wrong about who is at risk carries real cost. The central design decision here is to separate concerns: an agent-based model governs the mechanisms (social contagion, trust erosion, disclosure, appointment dynamics), while a machine learning layer handles prediction (near-term risk scores derived from recent agent histories). Neither component could do the other’s job well.

24.1 Model Architecture and the Separation of Roles

The model runs 400 agents over 104 simulated weeks on a Watts–Strogatz small-world network with mean degree 6 and rewiring probability 0.15. The network structure is not decorative; it encodes the empirically documented clustering of mental health trajectories within social groups (Cacioppo & Fowler, 2013; Rosenquist et al., 2011). Each agent carries continuous state variables — distress, support, trust, help-seeking propensity, stigma, and structural barriers — initialized from beta distributions that produce realistic right-skewed profiles rather than uniform or Gaussian starting conditions.

The ABM layer is responsible for all within-agent and between-agent dynamics: exogenous shock events, social contagion of distress, natural decay and recovery, disclosure decisions, referral chains, appointment attendance, and crisis events. These are modeled as probabilistic transitions governed by logistic functions of the current agent state, not learned policies. The ML layer sits above this: every four weeks, two Random Forest classifiers are retrained on the accumulated history of agent feature snapshots labeled with subsequent outcomes (no-show and crisis), and their output scores are used to prioritize outreach allocation. The two components share information but have no feedback from ML predictions back into agent state dynamics — which is an important architectural choice and a limitation discussed later.

24.2 Distress Dynamics and Social Contagion

The individual distress update combines three additive terms: a baseline vulnerability drift, a social support buffer, and an engaged-care benefit for fully enrolled agents. The deterministic core of the update is:

\[ d_{t+1} = d_t + \delta \cdot v_i - \sigma \cdot s_i - \beta \cdot \mathbf{1}[\text{eng}_i = 2] + \kappa \cdot \min(m_i, 3) \cdot 0.1 + \varepsilon_t \]

where \[\delta = 0.10\] is the distress decay rate, \[v_i\] is individual baseline vulnerability, \[\sigma = 0.18\] is the support buffer coefficient, \[\beta = 0.15\] is the engaged-care benefit, \[\kappa = 0.12\] is the missed-appointment penalty, and \[\varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.03)\] is process noise. The three-state engagement variable — 0 (disengaged), 1 (loosely connected), 2 (actively enrolled) — is the key moderator: only fully enrolled agents receive the care benefit, and repeated missed appointments degrade engagement state.

Social contagion is modeled as a neighborhood effect on the Watts–Strogatz graph:

def step_social(self):
    neighbors = list(self.model.G.neighbors(self.unique_id))
    nb_distress = [agent_map[n].distress for n in neighbors if n in agent_map]
    nb_support  = [agent_map[n].support  for n in neighbors if n in agent_map]
    contagion_effect = p["contagion_weight"] * max(np.mean(nb_distress) - 0.3, 0)
    support_effect   = p["support_buffer"]   * np.mean(nb_support) * 0.3
    self.distress    = clip01(self.distress + contagion_effect - support_effect)

The threshold of 0.3 in the contagion term means that low-distress neighborhoods are effectively neutral — only neighbors with distress meaningfully above the population median transmit upward pressure. This is consistent with the asymmetric contagion literature, where distress spreads more readily than recovery (Coyne, 1976; Joiner & Coyne, 1999). Dry run: an agent with distress at 0.45 surrounded by four neighbors averaging 0.55 receives a contagion increment of approximately \[0.10 \times (0.55 - 0.30) = 0.025\], partially offset by the support buffer. The effect is small per step but accumulates over weeks without intervention.

24.3 Disclosure, Referral, and Appointment Dynamics

All behavioral transitions are modeled via logistic functions of the relevant state variables. Disclosure probability reflects the literature on help-seeking barriers (Clement et al., 2015; Corrigan, 2004):

\[ P(\text{disclose}) = \sigma\!\left(-2.2 + 3.5 \cdot d_i + 1.5 \cdot h_i + 0.8 \cdot s_i - 1.2 \cdot \text{stig}_i\right) \]

where \[d_i\] is distress, \[h_i\] help-seeking propensity, \[s_i\] social support, and \[\text{stig}_i\] stigma. The dominant coefficient on distress reflects the well-documented pattern that individuals are most likely to seek help near crisis rather than at early symptom stages — a pattern that this model may inadvertently reinforce through the late-arriving reward structure. Attendance probability adds trust and engagement to the equation and subtracts barriers and distress:

\[ P(\text{attend}) = \sigma\!\left(1.5 + 1.5 \cdot \tau_i + 0.5 \cdot \text{eng}_i - 2.0 \cdot d_i - 2.0 \cdot b_i + 2.0 \cdot \text{out}_i\right) \]

where \[\tau_i\] is trust, \[b_i\] structural barriers, and \[\text{out}_i\] is an indicator for whether the agent received outreach that week. That outreach term — with a coefficient of 2.0 — is the main mechanism by which the allocation policy translates into attendance outcomes. When an agent attends, trust increases by 0.05 and the missed-appointment counter decrements; when they miss, trust drops by 0.08, the counter increments, and engagement can degrade. The asymmetry is intentional: trust is harder to build than to lose, which is consistent with the therapeutic alliance literature (Horvath & Symonds, 1991).

def step_attend(self):
    if not self.appt_was_scheduled:
        return False
    prob = sigmoid(
        p["attend_base"]
        + 1.5 * self.trust
        + 0.5 * float(self.engagement)
        - 2.0 * self.distress
        - 2.0 * self.barriers
        + 2.0 * float(self.outreach_this_week)
    )
    attended = bernoulli(prob)
    if attended:
        self.trust = clip01(self.trust + 0.05)
        self.recent_missed = max(0, self.recent_missed - 1)
    else:
        self.trust = clip01(self.trust - 0.08)
        self.recent_missed = min(self.recent_missed + 1, 8)
    return attended

Dry run: an agent with trust 0.5, engagement 1, distress 0.7, barriers 0.4, and outreach gives an attendance probability of \[\sigma(1.5 + 0.75 + 0.5 - 1.4 - 0.8 + 2.0) \approx \sigma(2.55) \approx 0.93\]. Without outreach, the same agent attends with probability \[\sigma(0.55) \approx 0.63\]. The gap is meaningful at the population level and is what makes targeted outreach allocation consequential.

24.4 The Machine Learning Layer

The feature vector for each agent at each time step encodes both current state and recent history:

def get_features(self):
    return [
        self.distress,
        float(np.mean(self.distress_history)),   # 4-week rolling mean
        float(self.recent_missed),
        float(self.engagement),
        self.trust,
        self.support,
        neighbor_distress,                        # mean neighbor distress
        frac_high_distress,                       # fraction of neighbors > 0.6
        float(self.recent_outreach),
        float(self.recent_disclosures),
        float(min(self.weeks_since_attended, 20)),
        self.baseline_vuln,
        self.barriers,
        self.stigma,
    ]

Two separate Random Forest classifiers predict no-show and crisis outcomes, retrained every four weeks on labeled snapshots from the previous steps. The combined risk score is a weighted average:

\[ \rho_i = w \cdot \hat{P}(\text{crisis}_i) + (1 - w) \cdot \hat{P}(\text{no-show}_i), \quad w = 0.55 \]

The weight of 0.55 places slightly more emphasis on crisis prediction, reflecting the asymmetric cost of missing a high-risk individual relative to wasting an outreach contact on a low-risk one. Before sufficient training data has accumulated (fewer than 80 labeled rows with at least 5 positive examples in each class), the model falls back to a simple heuristic:

\[ \hat{\rho}_i^{\text{heuristic}} = 0.55 \cdot d_i + 0.20 \cdot v_i \]

This cold-start fallback is worth noting: in real implementations, the warm-up period before a trained model is available can span several weeks or months, during which the system’s targeting quality is substantially degraded. The model makes this explicit rather than assuming a pre-trained classifier is available from week one.

24.5 Outreach Policy Variants

Four policies are compared across the full 104-week run. The none policy serves as a baseline with no active outreach. The universal policy ranks agents by raw distress and outreaches the top 25 at reduced intensity (0.7). The targeted policy uses the combined ML risk score to rank agents and outreaches the top 25 at full intensity (1.0). The tiered policy stratifies agents by risk threshold — high-risk agents (\[\rho_i \geq 0.65\]) receive full-intensity outreach, mid-risk agents (\[0.35 \leq \rho_i < 0.65\]) receive half-intensity outreach — and depletes capacity across strata in priority order:

elif self.policy == "tiered":
    high = sorted([a for a in agents if a.combined_risk >= ht],
                  key=lambda a: -a.combined_risk)
    mid  = sorted([a for a in agents if mt <= a.combined_risk < ht],
                  key=lambda a: -a.combined_risk)
    cap = K
    for a in high[:cap]:
        a.apply_outreach(intensity=1.0); cap -= 1
    for a in mid[:cap]:
        a.apply_outreach(intensity=0.5); cap -= 1

The tiered policy is the most realistic of the four — real outreach programs routinely differentiate contact intensity by risk level — and it is also the most sensitive to the quality of the underlying risk scores. If the Random Forest is poorly calibrated, the stratification thresholds will misclassify agents and the policy will underperform relative to the simpler distress-based universal approach. That conditional effectiveness is an important interpretive constraint on any simulation result.

24.6 Reading the Outputs and Limitations

The primary comparison metric is total crisis events across 104 weeks, supplemented by total missed appointments and final engagement rate. Weekly series are presented with a 4-week rolling average to reduce noise from the stochastic event structure. Feature importances from the no-show and crisis classifiers offer a secondary diagnostic: if the model is capturing realistic dynamics, distress trajectory features (current distress, rolling mean, weeks since attended) should dominate, with peer-level features (neighbor distress, fraction high-distress neighbors) contributing meaningfully but secondarily.

Several structural limitations should constrain how these results are read. The ML model is trained entirely on simulation-generated data, which means its learned patterns are only as realistic as the logistic functions that generated the outcomes. There is no feedback from ML predictions into agent dynamics — a predicted high-risk agent does not alter their own behavior based on being flagged, which is unrealistic in any implementation where patients are informed of their risk status. The 104-week run is also relatively short for learning stable policies; the first ~20 weeks of cold-start operation bias the cumulative outcomes toward the heuristic fallback, potentially understating the gap between ML-guided and non-ML-guided policies. Finally, the network is static across the full simulation. In practice, social networks around individuals experiencing mental health crises reorganize substantially over time (Corrigan & Miller, 2004), and a dynamic network would likely amplify both the contagion and the isolation dynamics that are currently underrepresented here.

What the model does contribute is a clean separation of mechanism from prediction that is uncommon in mental health simulation literature. Most ABMs in this domain either use purely rule-based agents (which cannot adapt to data) or embed full RL policies (which can, but at the cost of interpretability). Treating the ABM as a mechanistic engine and the ML layer as a prediction oracle is a more faithful representation of how these components might actually be deployed in a real service system — where clinicians need to trust the prediction model, not just the simulation that generated the training data, and where the causal mechanisms governing engagement and crisis remain partially opaque even with good predictive performance.

24.7 Extensions Worth Considering

The most consequential extension would be allowing trust and engagement to influence the feature importance structure over time — specifically, testing whether early outreach that successfully builds trust changes the relative predictive weight of social network features versus individual trajectory features. A second useful addition would be modeling the organizational side: appointment capacity and outreach staff are treated as fixed parameters here, but in real systems they fluctuate with funding cycles in ways that interact non-trivially with the risk score thresholds. A tiered policy calibrated to a capacity of 25 outreach contacts per week will perform very differently under sudden capacity reductions, and the simulation could generate informative stress tests of policy robustness. Finally, partially observable agent states — where trust or baseline vulnerability are not directly accessible to the ML feature vector, but must be inferred from behavioral proxies — would bring the prediction problem closer to the actual information environment of a mental health care system.

# ============================================================
# 2. UTILITY FUNCTIONS
# ============================================================
def sigmoid(x):
    return 1.0 / (1.0 + np.exp(-np.clip(x, -10, 10)))

def clip01(x):
    return float(np.clip(x, 0.0, 1.0))

def bernoulli(p):
    return np.random.rand() < p

# ============================================================
# 3. AGENT CLASS
# ============================================================
class AtRiskAgent(Agent):
    def __init__(self, model):
        super().__init__(model)
        p   = model.params
        rng = np.random

        self.baseline_vuln       = clip01(rng.beta(2, 5))
        self.distress            = clip01(rng.beta(2, 4) * 0.6 + self.baseline_vuln * 0.4)
        self.support             = clip01(rng.beta(4, 2))
        self.trust               = clip01(rng.beta(3, 2))
        self.help_seeking        = clip01(rng.beta(2, 3))
        self.stigma              = clip01(rng.beta(2, 4))
        self.barriers            = clip01(rng.beta(2, 4))

        r = rng.rand()
        if r < p["initial_engaged_frac"]:
            self.engagement = 2
        elif r < p["initial_engaged_frac"] + p["initial_loose_frac"]:
            self.engagement = 1
        else:
            self.engagement = 0

        self.has_appointment      = False
        self.attended_last        = False
        self.weeks_since_attended = rng.randint(0, 8)
        self.recent_missed        = 0
        self.recent_outreach      = 0
        self.recent_disclosures   = 0
        self.crisis_last_week     = False
        self.outreach_this_week   = False
        self.referral_this_week   = False
        self.crisis_this_week     = False
        self.appt_was_scheduled   = False

        self.distress_history = deque(
            [self.distress] * p["history_window"],
            maxlen=p["history_window"]
        )
        self.pred_no_show   = 0.3
        self.pred_crisis    = 0.2
        self.combined_risk  = 0.25

    def get_features(self):
        # Neighbor stats via model graph
        neighbors = list(self.model.G.neighbors(self.unique_id))
        agent_map = self.model.agent_map
        if neighbors:
            nb_distress = [agent_map[n].distress for n in neighbors if n in agent_map]
            neighbor_distress    = float(np.mean(nb_distress)) if nb_distress else 0.0
            frac_high_distress   = float(np.mean([d > 0.6 for d in nb_distress])) if nb_distress else 0.0
        else:
            neighbor_distress, frac_high_distress = 0.0, 0.0

        return [
            self.distress,
            float(np.mean(self.distress_history)),
            float(self.recent_missed),
            float(self.engagement),
            self.trust,
            self.support,
            neighbor_distress,
            frac_high_distress,
            float(self.recent_outreach),
            float(self.recent_disclosures),
            float(min(self.weeks_since_attended, 20)),
            self.baseline_vuln,
            self.barriers,
            self.stigma,
        ]

    def step_shocks(self):
        p = self.model.params
        if bernoulli(p["shock_prob"]):
            self.distress = clip01(self.distress + p["shock_magnitude"] * np.random.rand())

    def step_social(self):
        p         = self.model.params
        neighbors = list(self.model.G.neighbors(self.unique_id))
        agent_map = self.model.agent_map
        if not neighbors:
            return
        nb_distress = [agent_map[n].distress for n in neighbors if n in agent_map]
        nb_support  = [agent_map[n].support  for n in neighbors if n in agent_map]
        if not nb_distress:
            return
        contagion_effect = p["contagion_weight"] * max(float(np.mean(nb_distress)) - 0.3, 0)
        support_effect   = p["support_buffer"]   * float(np.mean(nb_support)) * 0.3
        self.distress    = clip01(self.distress + contagion_effect - support_effect)

    def step_distress(self):
        p = self.model.params
        d = self.distress
        d += p["distress_decay"] * self.baseline_vuln
        d -= p["support_buffer"] * self.support
        if self.engagement == 2:
            d -= p["engaged_care_benefit"]
        d += p["miss_penalty"] * min(self.recent_missed, 3) * 0.1
        d += np.random.normal(0, p["noise_sd"])
        self.distress = clip01(d)
        self.distress_history.append(self.distress)

    def step_disclosure(self):
        p    = self.model.params
        prob = sigmoid(
            p["disclosure_base"]
            + 3.5 * self.distress
            + 1.5 * self.help_seeking
            + 0.8 * self.support
            - 1.2 * self.stigma
        )
        disclosed = bernoulli(prob)
        if disclosed:
            self.recent_disclosures = min(self.recent_disclosures + 1, 5)
        return disclosed

    def step_referral(self, disclosed):
        p = self.model.params
        if not disclosed or self.engagement == 2:
            return False
        prob = sigmoid(
            p["referral_base"]
            + 3.0 * self.distress
            + 1.5 * self.trust
            - 0.5 * float(self.has_appointment)
        )
        return bernoulli(prob)

    def step_attend(self):
        if not self.appt_was_scheduled:
            return False
        p    = self.model.params
        prob = sigmoid(
            p["attend_base"]
            + 1.5 * self.trust
            + 0.5 * float(self.engagement)
            - 2.0 * self.distress
            - 2.0 * self.barriers
            + 2.0 * float(self.outreach_this_week)
        )
        attended = bernoulli(prob)
        if attended:
            self.attended_last        = True
            self.weeks_since_attended = 0
            self.trust                = clip01(self.trust + 0.05)
            self.recent_missed        = max(0, self.recent_missed - 1)
            if self.engagement < 2:
                self.engagement += 1
        else:
            self.attended_last        = False
            self.recent_missed        = min(self.recent_missed + 1, 8)
            self.trust                = clip01(self.trust - 0.08)
            self.weeks_since_attended += 1
            if self.engagement > 0 and self.recent_missed >= 2:
                self.engagement -= 1
        self.has_appointment = False
        return attended

    def step_crisis(self):
        p     = self.model.params
        prob  = sigmoid(
            p["crisis_base"]
            + 4.5 * self.distress
            + 1.8 * min(self.recent_missed, 4) * 0.2
            - 1.5 * self.support
            - 1.0 * float(self.outreach_this_week)
        )
        crisis = bernoulli(prob)
        self.crisis_this_week = crisis
        if crisis:
            self.distress = clip01(self.distress + 0.15)
            self.trust    = clip01(self.trust    - 0.05)
        return crisis

    def apply_outreach(self, intensity=1.0):
        p = self.model.params
        self.outreach_this_week = True
        self.recent_outreach    = min(self.recent_outreach + 1, 5)
        self.trust    = clip01(self.trust    + p["outreach_trust_boost"] * intensity)
        self.distress = clip01(self.distress - p["outreach_distress_cut"] * intensity)

    def reset_weekly(self):
        self.outreach_this_week   = False
        self.referral_this_week   = False
        self.crisis_last_week     = self.crisis_this_week
        self.crisis_this_week     = False
        self.appt_was_scheduled   = False
        self.attended_last        = False
        self.weeks_since_attended += 1
        self.recent_missed        = max(0, self.recent_missed - 0.05)
        self.recent_outreach      = max(0, self.recent_outreach - 0.2)
        self.recent_disclosures   = max(0, self.recent_disclosures - 0.3)

# 4. MODEL CLASS
# ============================================================
class HybridMentalHealthModel(Model):
    def __init__(self, params, policy="none", seed=SEED):
        super().__init__(seed=seed)
        np.random.seed(seed)
        self.params  = params
        self.policy  = policy
        self.week    = 0

        # Social network
        self.G = nx.watts_strogatz_graph(
            params["N"], params["network_k"], params["network_p"], seed=seed
        )

        # Create agents (Mesa 3.x: pass model as first arg)
        for _ in range(params["N"]):
            AtRiskAgent(self)

        # Build uid -> agent map for fast neighbor lookup
        self.agent_map = {a.unique_id: a for a in self.agents}

        # Relabel network to match agent unique_ids
        uid_list = list(self.agent_map.keys())
        mapping  = {i: uid_list[i] for i in range(params["N"])}
        self.G   = nx.relabel_nodes(self.G, mapping)

        # ML infrastructure
        self.clf_no_show   = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=80, random_state=seed)
        self.clf_crisis    = RandomForestClassifier(n_estimators=80, random_state=seed)
        self.scaler        = StandardScaler()
        self.ml_trained    = False
        self.training_rows = []
        self._prev_snapshots = []

        # Logs
        self.weekly_crisis     = []
        self.weekly_missed     = []
        self.weekly_engaged    = []
        self.weekly_referrals  = []
        self.weekly_outreached = []

        self.datacollector = DataCollector(
            model_reporters={
                "WeeklyCrisis"    : lambda m: m._w_crisis,
                "WeeklyMissed"    : lambda m: m._w_missed,
                "EngagementRate"  : lambda m: m._w_engaged,
            }
        )
        self._w_crisis  = 0
        self._w_missed  = 0
        self._w_engaged = 0

    # ---- ML helpers ----
    def _snapshot_features(self):
        return [
            {"uid": a.unique_id, "week": self.week,
             "feats": a.get_features(), "no_show": None, "crisis": None}
            for a in self.agents
        ]

    def _label_rows(self, snapshots):
        labeled = []
        for row in snapshots:
            a = self.agent_map.get(row["uid"])
            if a is None:
                continue
            row = row.copy()
            row["no_show"] = int(a.appt_was_scheduled and not a.attended_last)
            row["crisis"]  = int(a.crisis_last_week)
            labeled.append(row)
        return labeled

    def _retrain(self):
        if len(self.training_rows) < 80:
            return
        feats     = np.array([r["feats"]    for r in self.training_rows])
        labels_ns = np.array([r["no_show"]  for r in self.training_rows])
        labels_cr = np.array([r["crisis"]   for r in self.training_rows])
        if labels_ns.sum() < 5 or labels_cr.sum() < 5:
            return
        feats_s = self.scaler.fit_transform(feats)
        self.clf_no_show.fit(feats_s, labels_ns)
        self.clf_crisis.fit(feats_s,  labels_cr)
        self.ml_trained = True

    def _score_agents(self):
        w      = self.params["risk_weight_crisis"]
        agents = list(self.agents)
        feats  = np.array([a.get_features() for a in agents])
        if self.ml_trained:
            fs   = self.scaler.transform(feats)
            p_ns = self.clf_no_show.predict_proba(fs)[:, 1]
            p_cr = self.clf_crisis.predict_proba(fs)[:,  1]
        else:
            p_ns = np.array([a.distress * 0.5 + a.recent_missed * 0.05 for a in agents])
            p_cr = np.array([a.distress * 0.6 + a.baseline_vuln * 0.2  for a in agents])
        for i, a in enumerate(agents):
            a.pred_no_show  = float(np.clip(p_ns[i], 0, 1))
            a.pred_crisis   = float(np.clip(p_cr[i], 0, 1))
            a.combined_risk = float(np.clip(w * p_cr[i] + (1 - w) * p_ns[i], 0, 1))

    # ---- Outreach policies ----
    def _allocate_outreach(self):
        K       = self.params["outreach_capacity"]
        ht      = self.params["high_risk_threshold"]
        mt      = self.params["mid_risk_threshold"]
        agents  = list(self.agents)

        if self.policy == "none":
            pass

        elif self.policy == "universal":
            cands = sorted(agents, key=lambda a: -a.distress)
            for a in cands[:K]:
                a.apply_outreach(intensity=0.7)

        elif self.policy == "targeted":
            cands = sorted(agents, key=lambda a: -a.combined_risk)
            for a in cands[:K]:
                a.apply_outreach(intensity=1.0)

        elif self.policy == "tiered":
            high = sorted(
                [a for a in agents if a.combined_risk >= ht],
                key=lambda a: -a.combined_risk
            )
            mid  = sorted(
                [a for a in agents if mt <= a.combined_risk < ht],
                key=lambda a: -a.combined_risk
            )
            cap = K
            for a in high[:cap]:
                a.apply_outreach(intensity=1.0); cap -= 1
            for a in mid[:cap]:
                a.apply_outreach(intensity=0.5); cap -= 1

    def _schedule_appointments(self, referrals):
        cap  = self.params["appointment_capacity"]
        cands = [a for a in referrals if not a.has_appointment]
        np.random.shuffle(cands)
        for a in cands[:cap]:
            a.has_appointment    = True
            a.appt_was_scheduled = True

    # ---- Main model step ----
    def step(self):
        agents = list(self.agents)

        # Label rows from last week
        if self.week > 0:
            labeled = self._label_rows(self._prev_snapshots)
            self.training_rows.extend(labeled)

        # Retrain ML periodically
        if self.week % self.params["retrain_every"] == 0:
            self._retrain()

        # Score agents
        self._score_agents()

        # Outreach (before agents act)
        self._allocate_outreach()

        # Shocks
        for a in agents: a.step_shocks()

        # Social contagion / support
        for a in agents: a.step_social()

        # Distress update
        for a in agents: a.step_distress()

        # Disclosure & referral
        referrals = []
        for a in agents:
            disclosed = a.step_disclosure()
            if a.step_referral(disclosed):
                referrals.append(a)
                a.referral_this_week = True

        # Appointment scheduling
        self._schedule_appointments(referrals)

        # Attendance
        w_missed = 0
        for a in agents:
            attended = a.step_attend()
            if a.appt_was_scheduled and not attended:
                w_missed += 1

        # Crisis events
        w_crisis = sum(a.step_crisis() for a in agents)

        # Logs
        n_engaged   = sum(1 for a in agents if a.engagement == 2)
        n_outreached = sum(1 for a in agents if a.outreach_this_week)

        self.weekly_crisis.append(w_crisis)
        self.weekly_missed.append(w_missed)
        self.weekly_engaged.append(n_engaged / len(agents))
        self.weekly_referrals.append(len(referrals))
        self.weekly_outreached.append(n_outreached)

        self._w_crisis  = w_crisis
        self._w_missed  = w_missed
        self._w_engaged = n_engaged / len(agents)
        self.datacollector.collect(self)

        # Snapshot for labeling next week
        self._prev_snapshots = self._snapshot_features()

        # Reset weekly flags
        for a in agents: a.reset_weekly()

        self.week += 1

    def run(self):
        self._prev_snapshots = self._snapshot_features()
        for _ in range(self.params["weeks"]):
            self.step()

    print(f"  Running policy: {policy} ...")

# ============================================================
# MICRO LEVEL: Individual Employee
# ============================================================
class Employee(Agent):
    """
    Individual agent with mental health state variables.

    Mental health dynamics are grounded in the workload-control model:
      - stress rises with workload and social contagion from team
      - social support buffers stress accumulation
      - resilience buffers depression onset
      - cross-level constraints: team stress (meso) and department
        budget (macro) feed top-down into individual dynamics

    References:
      Karasek (1979) demand-control model;
      Siegrist (1996) effort-reward imbalance model;
      ABM reviews by Tracy et al. (PMC10030380) and Galea et al. (PMC6284360)
    """

    def __init__(self, unique_id, model, team_id, department_id):
        super().__init__(unique_id, model)
        self.team_id        = team_id
        self.department_id  = department_id

        self.stress          = np.random.uniform(20, 50)
        self.depression_risk = np.random.uniform(10, 30)
        self.social_support  = np.random.uniform(30, 70)
        self.resilience      = np.random.uniform(40, 80)
        self.workload        = np.random.uniform(30, 60)

        self.stress_history     = [self.stress]
        self.depression_history = [self.depression_risk]
        self.connections        = []   # list of Employee unique_ids

    def step(self):
        team = self.model.team_registry[self.team_id]
        dept = self.model.dept_registry[self.department_id]

        # --- bottom-up: contribute to team sums (team resets them before calling step)
        team.workload_sum          += self.workload

        # --- top-down: macro and meso constraints
        dept_budget_factor  = dept.budget / 100.0
        team_stress_avg     = team.stress_average

        workload_stress = self.workload * 0.30
        contagion_stress = team_stress_avg * 0.15        # stress contagion (Hatfield 1993)
        budget_stress   = (1 - dept_budget_factor) * 20  # resource scarcity effect

        support_buffer   = self.social_support * 0.25
        resilience_buffer = self.resilience * 0.30

        stress_delta = workload_stress + contagion_stress + budget_stress - support_buffer
        self.stress = np.clip(
            self.stress + stress_delta + np.random.normal(0, 3), 0, 100
        )

        dep_delta = self.stress * 0.25 - resilience_buffer - self.social_support * 0.15
        self.depression_risk = np.clip(
            self.depression_risk + dep_delta + np.random.normal(0, 2), 0, 100
        )

        # social support diffusion over personal network
        if self.connections:
            neighbour_support = np.mean(
                [self.model.employee_registry[c].social_support
                 for c in self.connections
                 if c in self.model.employee_registry]
            )
            self.social_support = np.clip(
                self.social_support * 0.95 + neighbour_support * 0.05, 0, 100
            )

        self.workload = np.clip(self.workload + np.random.normal(0, 5), 0, 100)

        self.stress_history.append(self.stress)
        self.depression_history.append(self.depression_risk)

        # bottom-up: feed into team sums
        team.employee_stress_sum     += self.stress
        team.employee_depression_sum += self.depression_risk


# ============================================================
# MESO LEVEL: Team
# ============================================================
class Team(Agent):
    """
    Team-level agent — implements the group/module layer in multi-level Mesa.

    Scheduling order within each step:
      1. Reset accumulators
      2. Step all employees      (bottom-up data generation)
      3. Aggregate to team level (emergent team state)
      4. Apply team dynamics     (group-level processes)
      5. Push feedback downward  (top-down constraints)

    This mirrors the Department pattern provided in the course brief.
    """

    def __init__(self, unique_id, model, department_id):
        super().__init__(unique_id, model)
        self.department_id = department_id
        self.employees: list[Employee] = []

        self.stress_average  = 0.0
        self.support_level   = np.random.uniform(40, 70)
        self.department_budget = 50.0   # updated top-down by Department each step

        # accumulators — reset at start of each step
        self.workload_sum          = 0.0
        self.employee_stress_sum   = 0.0
        self.employee_depression_sum = 0.0

        self.stress_history  = [self.stress_average]
        self.support_history = [self.support_level]

    def add_employee(self, emp: Employee):
        self.employees.append(emp)

    def step(self):
        # 1. reset
        self.workload_sum          = 0.0
        self.employee_stress_sum   = 0.0
        self.employee_depression_sum = 0.0

        # 2. step employees
        for emp in self.employees:
            emp.step()

        # 3. aggregate
        n = len(self.employees)
        if n:
            self.stress_average = self.employee_stress_sum / n
            avg_workload        = self.workload_sum / n
        else:
            self.stress_average = 0.0
            avg_workload        = 0.0

        # 4. team-level dynamics
        team_support_buffer = self.support_level * 0.20
        workload_stress     = avg_workload * 0.20
        budget_stress       = (1 - self.department_budget / 100.0) * 15.0

        self.stress_average = np.clip(
            self.stress_average * 0.9
            + workload_stress + budget_stress
            - team_support_buffer
            + np.random.normal(0, 2),
            0, 100
        )

        if n:
            avg_emp_support  = np.mean([e.social_support for e in self.employees])
            self.support_level = np.clip(
                self.support_level * 0.95 + avg_emp_support * 0.05, 0, 100
            )

        # 5. top-down: push updated team stress back to employees
        for emp in self.employees:
            emp.team_stress_avg = self.stress_average

        self.stress_history.append(self.stress_average)
        self.support_history.append(self.support_level)

# MACRO LEVEL: Department
# ============================================================
class Department(Agent):
    """
    Department-level agent — top of the hierarchy.

    Mirrors the Department pattern from the course brief:
        self.employees → replaced by self.teams
        self.budget    → institutional resource level
        step()         → steps teams, updates dept metrics,
                         pushes budget top-down

    Budget undergoes a random walk with a penalty when organisational
    stress is high, consistent with literature showing high employee
    stress correlates with reduced organisational performance.
    """

    def __init__(self, unique_id, model):
        super().__init__(unique_id, model)
        self.teams: list[Team] = []
        self.budget = np.random.uniform(50, 100)

        self.stress_average      = 0.0
        self.mental_health_score = 50.0

        self.budget_history          = [self.budget]
        self.stress_history          = [self.stress_average]
        self.mental_health_history   = [self.mental_health_score]

    def add_team(self, team: Team):
        self.teams.append(team)

    def update_department_metrics(self):
        """Aggregate upward and update institutional state."""
        if self.teams:
            self.stress_average = np.mean([t.stress_average for t in self.teams])
            avg_support         = np.mean([t.support_level for t in self.teams])
        else:
            self.stress_average = 0.0
            avg_support         = 0.0

        budget_change = np.random.normal(0, 3)
        if self.stress_average > 60:
            budget_change -= 2          # high stress erodes budget efficiency
        self.budget = np.clip(self.budget + budget_change, 0, 100)

        self.mental_health_score = np.clip(
            100 - self.stress_average + avg_support * 0.30, 0, 100
        )

        # push budget down to teams
        for t in self.teams:
            t.department_budget = self.budget

        self.budget_history.append(self.budget)
        self.stress_history.append(self.stress_average)
        self.mental_health_history.append(self.mental_health_score)

    def step(self):
        for team in self.teams:
            team.step()
        self.update_department_metrics()

# MODEL
# ============================================================
class MentalHealthModel(Model):
    """
    Multi-level ABM for workplace mental health.

    Hierarchy:
        Macro  — Department   (institutional budget, org-level stress)
        Meso   — Team         (group stress, support culture)
        Micro  — Employee     (individual stress, depression risk, resilience)

    Cross-scale dynamics:
        Bottom-up: employee workload/stress → team average → dept average
        Top-down:  dept budget → team budget → individual budget stress
                   team stress → individual stress contagion

    Social network:
        Within-team ties: p = 0.70   (dense, peer support)
        Cross-team ties:  p = 0.15   (sparser, organisational bridging)
    """

    def __init__(self, num_departments=3, num_teams_per_dept=2, num_employees_per_team=5):
        super().__init__()

        self.num_departments       = num_departments
        self.num_teams_per_dept    = num_teams_per_dept
        self.num_employees_per_team = num_employees_per_team

        # Use distinct names to avoid Mesa reserved attribute collision
        self.dept_registry:     OrderedDict[int, Department] = OrderedDict()
        self.team_registry:     OrderedDict[int, Team]       = OrderedDict()
        self.employee_registry: OrderedDict[int, Employee]   = OrderedDict()

        self._build_hierarchy()
        self._build_social_network()

        # Scheduler: only departments are registered;
        # they cascade steps to teams → employees internally.
        self.scheduler = RandomActivation(self)
        for dept in self.dept_registry.values():
            self.scheduler.add(dept)

        self.datacollector = DataCollector(
            model_reporters={
                "avg_stress":     lambda m: m._model_avg("stress"),
                "avg_depression": lambda m: m._model_avg("depression_risk"),
                "avg_support":    lambda m: m._model_avg("social_support"),
            },
            agent_reporters={
                "stress":          "stress",
                "depression_risk": "depression_risk",
                "social_support":  "social_support",
                "resilience":      "resilience",
            }
        )

    # ----------------------------------------------------------
    def _build_hierarchy(self):
        for dept_id in range(self.num_departments):
            dept = Department(dept_id, self)
            self.dept_registry[dept_id] = dept

            for ti in range(self.num_teams_per_dept):
                team_id = self.num_teams_per_dept * dept_id + ti
                team = Team(team_id, self, dept_id)
                self.team_registry[team_id] = team
                dept.add_team(team)

                for ei in range(self.num_employees_per_team):
                    emp_id = self.num_employees_per_team * team_id + ei
                    emp = Employee(emp_id, self, team_id, dept_id)
                    self.employee_registry[emp_id] = emp
                    team.add_employee(emp)

    def _build_social_network(self):
        all_emps = list(self.employee_registry.values())

        # within-team dense ties
        for team in self.team_registry.values():
            es = team.employees
            for i in range(len(es)):
                for j in range(i + 1, len(es)):
                    if np.random.random() < 0.70:
                        es[i].connections.append(es[j].unique_id)
                        es[j].connections.append(es[i].unique_id)

        # cross-team weak ties
        for i in range(len(all_emps)):
            for j in range(i + 1, len(all_emps)):
                if (all_emps[i].team_id != all_emps[j].team_id
                        and np.random.random() < 0.15):
                    all_emps[i].connections.append(all_emps[j].unique_id)
                    all_emps[j].connections.append(all_emps[i].unique_id)

    # ----------------------------------------------------------
    def _model_avg(self, attr: str) -> float:
        emps = list(self.employee_registry.values())
        return float(np.mean([getattr(e, attr) for e in emps])) if emps else 0.0

    def step(self):
        self.scheduler.step()
        self.datacollector.collect(self)

    def run_model(self, n_steps: int):
        for _ in range(n_steps):
            self.step()

    # ----------------------------------------------------------
    def get_employee_df(self) -> pd.DataFrame:
        rows = []
        for e in self.employee_registry.values():
            rows.append({
                "employee_id":        e.unique_id,
                "team_id":            e.team_id,
                "department_id":      e.department_id,
                "final_stress":       e.stress,
                "final_depression":   e.depression_risk,
                "final_support":      e.social_support,
                "final_resilience":   e.resilience,
                "avg_stress":         float(np.mean(e.stress_history)),
                "avg_depression":     float(np.mean(e.depression_history)),
            })
        return pd.DataFrame(rows)

    def get_team_df(self) -> pd.DataFrame:
        rows = []
        for t in self.team_registry.values():
            rows.append({
                "team_id":            t.unique_id,
                "department_id":      t.department_id,
                "n_employees":        len(t.employees),
                "final_stress_avg":   t.stress_average,
                "final_support":      t.support_level,
                "avg_stress":         float(np.mean(t.stress_history)),
            })
        return pd.DataFrame(rows)

    def get_department_df(self) -> pd.DataFrame:
        rows = []
        for d in self.dept_registry.values():
            rows.append({
                "department_id":      d.unique_id,
                "n_teams":            len(d.teams),
                "final_budget":       d.budget,
                "final_stress_avg":   d.stress_average,
                "mental_health_score":d.mental_health_score,
                "avg_budget":         float(np.mean(d.budget_history)),
                "avg_stress":         float(np.mean(d.stress_history)),
            })
        return pd.DataFrame(rows)
