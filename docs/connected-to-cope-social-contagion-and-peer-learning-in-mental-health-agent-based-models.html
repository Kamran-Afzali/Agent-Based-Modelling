Agent Based Modelling

Chapter 18 Connected to Cope: Social Contagion and Peer Learning in Mental Health Agent-Based Models

Our previous model placed adaptive Q-learning agents alongside fixed-strategy agents in a shared environment and watched them navigate psychological stress. Both types faced the same Ornstein-Uhlenbeck daily stress process, the same fluctuating support availability, and the same three coping actions. The Q-learners gradually outperformed their rigid counterparts by discovering context-sensitive policies — seeking support when it was available, defaulting to healthy coping when it wasn’t, and learning through painful experience to distrust avoidance. But those agents lived in social isolation. They adapted to the environment, not to each other.

That isolation was always a simplification. Human coping does not happen in a vacuum. Stress spreads through friendships; a colleague’s bad week bleeds into yours. Support is shared; the resilience of those around us shapes how supported we feel. And coping strategies propagate socially — watching someone successfully manage a hard week nudges us toward their approach. Version 2 of the model introduces all three of these mechanisms, transforming a collection of independent learners into an interconnected social network. The consequences, as we will see, reach far beyond what individual adaptation alone could produce.

18.1 Three New Social Forces

Version 2 adds three agent-level mechanisms that operate on every step, layered on top of the individual MDP dynamics from before:

  • Stress contagion — each agent’s stress is partially pulled toward its neighbours’ average, with a contagion coefficient of 0.08. Neighbours who are more stressed transmit some of that load; neighbours who are calmer provide a modest buffer.
  • Support sharing — perceived social support is similarly nudged toward the neighbourhood average at a rate of 0.05. This captures how emotionally supportive social environments lift individual wellbeing even without explicit help-seeking.
  • Peer Q-learning — each Q-learning agent identifies the neighbour with the highest cumulative lifetime reward and, if that neighbour is outperforming them, soft-blends its Q-table entries toward theirs at a rate of 0.05. Fixed agents participate in contagion and support sharing but cannot copy Q-tables; they experience social effects passively.

Together, these three forces mean that an individual agent’s psychological trajectory is now a function of both its own learning and the composition and behaviour of its social neighbourhood.

18.2 The Social Network Structure

The model builds an Erdős–Rényi-style random network after all agents are instantiated, assigning each agent exactly N_NEIGHBOURS = 3 connections sampled without replacement from the full population:

all_agents = list(self.agents)
for agent in all_agents:
    candidates = [a for a in all_agents if a is not agent]
    agent.neighbours = random.sample(candidates, min(N_NEIGHBOURS, len(candidates)))

This design is intentionally minimal — undirected, unweighted, fixed throughout the simulation. It suffices to demonstrate social dynamics without introducing the additional complexity of network evolution. Each Q-learner will have, on average, some proportion of connections to other Q-learners (peer learning links) and some to fixed agents (who contribute contagion and support but not Q-table knowledge). The asymmetry matters: a Q-learner surrounded mostly by fixed avoidance-biased neighbours will absorb elevated stress through contagion, while one embedded in a cluster of healthy-coping neighbours gains a double benefit — lower ambient stress and support buffering.

18.3 Reward Augmentation: Social Support as Intrinsic Value

The reward function gains a new term in v2:

\[ r_t = -\text{stress}_t + 0.5(\text{stress}_{t-1} - \text{stress}_t) + 0.02 \times \text{perceived\_support}_t \]

def compute_reward(self):
    delta         = self.prev_stress - self.stress
    support_bonus = self.perceived_support * 0.02
    return -self.stress + 0.5 * delta + support_bonus

The 0.02 coefficient is small but meaningful. It means agents now have an intrinsic motivation to maintain high perceived support — not just to reduce stress. Over time, this nudges the learned policy toward actions that build social capital (healthy coping, seek support) relative to avoidance, which provides stress relief but contributes nothing to the support dimension and incurs the familiar rebound cost. The support bonus interacts with stress contagion in a subtle way: agents in supportive neighbourhoods receive a support lift passively, which raises their reward signal, which in turn shapes Q-value estimates across states. The social environment seeps into the agent’s learned model of the world.

18.4 Peer Q-Learning: Observational Learning as a Computational Mechanism

The most novel mechanism is the Q-table soft-update from better-performing neighbours:

def social_interact(self):
    ql_neighbours = [n for n in self.neighbours if isinstance(n, QLearningCopingAgent)]
    if ql_neighbours:
        best_nbr = max(ql_neighbours, key=lambda n: n.cumulative_reward)
        if best_nbr.cumulative_reward > self.cumulative_reward:
            for key, nbr_q in best_nbr.q_table.items():
                own_q = self.q_table.get(key, 0.0)
                self.q_table[key] = own_q + PEER_LEARNING_RATE * (nbr_q - own_q)

This implements a soft-update analogous to Polyak averaging in deep RL target networks. Rather than wholesale copying a neighbour’s policy, each agent slowly blends toward it — preserving its own experience while incorporating vicarious information. The gating condition (best_nbr.cumulative_reward > self.cumulative_reward) ensures that only upward learning occurs; agents do not regress toward worse performers. smythos

The mechanism has a meaningful psychological analogue. Bandura’s social learning theory holds that humans learn coping strategies partly through observation — watching how others manage stress and updating their own behavioral repertoire accordingly. The peer Q-learning update formalizes this as a weighted average in value-function space, producing a computational account of observational learning that operates in parallel with direct experience. pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih

18.5 Stress Contagion: When Social Networks Hurt and Help

Stress contagion is bidirectional in the model: if your neighbours are more stressed than you, your stress rises; if they are calmer, yours falls. The net effect on a population depends critically on the distribution of initial stress and the topology of the network.

stress_delta = (avg_nbr_stress - self.stress)
self.stress += STRESS_CONTAGION * stress_delta

This creates a homogenization pressure. Over time, agents within the same social neighbourhood tend toward similar stress levels — a computational analogue of emotional contagion documented in empirical network studies. A key observable consequence is stress variance reduction: as the simulation progresses, the within-group variance of stress levels should decline, particularly in populations where agents share neighbourhoods and respond similarly. The dashboard’s Panel 5 tracks this directly through StressVar_QL and StressVar_Fixed. nature

The contagion coefficient of 0.08 is deliberately modest. At this level, social influence shapes trajectories over dozens of steps without overwhelming individual action dynamics. A higher coefficient would produce rapid convergence to a single population stress level, erasing the individual differences that make the agent comparison meaningful. A lower coefficient would make social effects invisible in a 200-step simulation. The parameter choice reflects a calibration judgment about the timescale at which social influences operate relative to individual coping.

Panel 5 is particularly diagnostic. A declining stress variance among Q-learners suggests that peer Q-learning and contagion are synchronizing the population — agents converging toward similar learned policies and similar stress equilibria. If variance among fixed agents declines faster, it implies that contagion dominates over policy learning as a homogenization force. If Q-learner variance declines more slowly, it may reflect the diversity-preserving effect of different ε trajectories: agents in different parts of their exploration-exploitation schedule receiving different peer signals.

Panel 6 tracks cumulative reward — the running sum of \(r_t\) across all steps. This serves as a proxy for lifetime psychological wellbeing under the model’s reward structure. Because the reward now includes the support bonus, agents who successfully build and maintain perceived support (through seek_support or healthy_coping) accumulate reward faster than those who repeatedly choose avoidance. The cumulative reward gap between Q-learners and fixed agents tells a more complete story than instantaneous stress alone: even if stress levels converge at some point, the path-dependent accumulated wellbeing may diverge substantially.

18.7 Emergent Dynamics: Clustering and Cascades

The interaction of contagion, support sharing, and peer Q-learning can produce emergent dynamics that neither mechanism generates alone. Consider a cluster of Q-learners who have independently converged on healthy coping as their dominant strategy. Their stress levels are moderate and declining. Through contagion, they exert a calming influence on neighbouring fixed agents. Those fixed agents, with lower stress, experience less environmental pressure and may select avoidance less frequently (since their stress levels now rarely cross thresholds that make immediate relief urgent). Meanwhile, the Q-learning cluster exchanges Q-table entries, accelerating convergence to similar policies.

This dynamic has the structure of a positive feedback loop in social health: adaptive individuals who find effective strategies partially extend those benefits to their social neighbours through contagion and support, even without explicit imitation. The reverse can also emerge: a cluster of avoidance-dominant fixed agents generates elevated ambient stress through contagion, which raises the effective environmental stress experienced by neighbouring Q-learners, making their optimization problem harder and potentially slowing convergence to optimal policies. scirp

These cascade dynamics — beneficial or harmful — are invisible when agents are modelled in isolation. They are the principal scientific contribution of the social extension.

18.8 The Asymmetry of Social Learning

Fixed agents participate in contagion and support sharing but cannot perform peer Q-learning. This asymmetry is intentional and psychologically meaningful. It models the difference between passive social influence (absorbing others’ emotional states, benefiting from their support) and active behavioral learning (consciously updating one’s coping strategies by observing what works for others).

The asymmetry creates a testable prediction: Q-learners embedded in neighbourhoods with other Q-learners should outperform Q-learners isolated in fixed-agent clusters, even controlling for their individual learning rates. The peer Q-learning mechanism provides an accelerant — it lets agents shortcut the exploration phase by inheriting partially-learned Q-values from better-performing social contacts. An isolated Q-learner must discover through trial and error that healthy coping outperforms avoidance in moderate-stress states; a connected Q-learner may learn this faster by observing a high-performing neighbour’s Q-table.

This suggests an intervention logic: in populations where some individuals struggle to discover effective coping strategies (slow convergence due to high environmental noise, low exploration, or maladaptive initialization), strategic social connections to already-adapted individuals could accelerate learning. Group therapy, peer support programs, and mentorship relationships all have this structure — connecting struggling individuals to those who have already developed effective coping repertoires. pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih

18.9 Methodological Considerations

Several modeling choices warrant scrutiny. The fixed neighbourhood assumption — agents maintain the same three connections for all 200 steps — ignores the dynamics of real social networks, where relationships form, strengthen, weaken, and dissolve. In reality, people often gravitate toward others with similar stress levels (homophily) or seek out resilient individuals when distressed; both processes would alter the contagion and peer learning dynamics substantially.

The symmetric contagion model assumes equal bidirectional influence. In practice, emotional contagion is asymmetric: high-distress individuals tend to have a disproportionate influence on their social environment relative to low-distress individuals. Implementing asymmetric contagion coefficients — where stressed agents transmit more than they receive — would produce more realistic cascade dynamics. pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih

The peer Q-learning mechanism uses cumulative reward as the selection criterion for whose Q-table to copy. Cumulative reward is a global quantity that agents cannot directly observe in most real settings — people observe outcomes, not utility totals. A more cognitively realistic version might use recent stress trajectory or explicit wellbeing signals as the comparison criterion, making the observational learning mechanism more locally informed.

18.10 Connections to Computational Social Science

The model now sits at the intersection of three research traditions. From computational psychiatry, it inherits the RL framework for modeling adaptive coping and the MDP formalization of psychological states. From agent-based social simulation, it inherits the emphasis on emergent population-level dynamics from local interaction rules. And from network science, it inherits the recognition that social topology shapes the diffusion of both stress and adaptive strategies. psychomodels

This convergence is increasingly characteristic of serious mental health modeling efforts. Real clinical interventions do not target isolated individuals but individuals embedded in families, peer networks, communities, and institutions. A model that treats agents as socially embedded — even in the minimal form implemented here — generates richer and more clinically relevant hypotheses than one that does not. Whether the social extension’s complexity earns its keep in explanatory power, predictive accuracy, or intervention insight is ultimately an empirical question. But it is a question the v1 model could not even ask.

18.11 From Isolated Learners to a Social Ecology of Coping

Version 1 demonstrated that adaptive agents outperform rigid ones when both face the same environment. Version 2 shows that the environment itself is partly constituted by other agents. Q-learners no longer face a stationary background; they face a social ecology whose properties depend on who their neighbours are, what strategies those neighbours have learned, and how effectively stress and support flow through the network.

This shift echoes a broader movement in behavioral science away from models of the isolated individual and toward models of the socially embedded agent. Coping is not just a skill that individuals develop in response to adversity; it is a capacity that is socially distributed, transmitted, and amplified or undermined by the networks in which people are embedded. Agent-based models with social interaction mechanisms provide one of the few computational tools capable of capturing this embeddedness while retaining the individual-level resolution necessary to study learning and adaptation. smythos

The journey from random walks through Schelling preferences to individual Q-learning to socially networked adaptive agents traces an arc of increasing realism. Each step has added complexity, but each has also added explanatory reach — revealing dynamics that the simpler model was constitutionally unable to produce. The question for future development is not whether to add more complexity, but which complexities most constrain and enrich our understanding of how people — and the communities they form — learn to cope.

# MENTAL HEALTH COPING SIMULATION — v2
# Mesa ABM + Q-Learning (MDP) + Agent-Agent Interaction
# Run as a single cell in Google Colab
# ============================================================

# ---- 1. Install / imports ----
# !pip install mesa -q   # uncomment in Colab if needed

import random
import math
import collections
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
import matplotlib.gridspec as gridspec
import mesa

print("Mesa version:", mesa.__version__)

# ============================================================
# 2. GLOBAL SETTINGS
# ============================================================
ACTIONS        = ["healthy_coping", "avoidance", "seek_support"]
LEARNING_RATE  = 0.15
DISCOUNT       = 0.95
EPSILON_START  = 1.0
EPSILON_MIN    = 0.05
EPSILON_DECAY  = 0.995

# Social-interaction parameters
STRESS_CONTAGION   = 0.08   # fraction of neighbour stress gap transmitted each step
SUPPORT_SHARING    = 0.05   # fraction of neighbour perceived_support shared
PEER_LEARNING_RATE = 0.05   # blending weight when copying a better neighbour Q-value
N_NEIGHBOURS       = 3      # how many social connections each agent maintains

def discretize(value, bin_size, max_bin=4):
    return max(0, min(int(value / bin_size), max_bin))


# ============================================================
# 3. Q-LEARNING COPING AGENT  (with social interaction)
# ============================================================
class QLearningCopingAgent(mesa.Agent):
    """
    State:  (stress_bin, daily_stress_bin, support_bin)
    Action: healthy_coping | avoidance | seek_support
    Reward: –stress + improvement bonus + social support bonus

    Social mechanisms
    -----------------
    * Stress contagion   – neighbour stress bleeds in/out
    * Support sharing    – neighbours partially lift perceived support
    * Peer Q-learning    – observe a better-performing neighbour and
                           soft-update own Q-values toward theirs
    """

    def __init__(self, model, learning_rate=LEARNING_RATE,
                 discount=DISCOUNT, epsilon=EPSILON_START):
        super().__init__(model)
        self.stress            = random.uniform(30, 70)
        self.perceived_support = random.uniform(30, 70)
        self.actions           = ACTIONS
        self.q_table           = {}
        self.learning_rate     = learning_rate
        self.discount          = discount
        self.epsilon           = epsilon
        self.memory            = collections.deque(maxlen=100)
        self.agent_type        = "Q-Learner"
        self.last_action       = None
        self.prev_stress       = self.stress
        self.neighbours        = []      # filled after all agents created
        self.cumulative_reward = 0.0     # tracks lifetime reward for peer comparison

    # ---------- MDP ----------
    def get_state(self):
        s_bin   = discretize(self.stress, 20)
        d_bin   = discretize(self.model.daily_stress, 5)
        sup_bin = discretize(self.perceived_support, 20)
        return (s_bin, d_bin, sup_bin)

    def update_q_value(self, state, action, reward, next_state):
        old_q    = self.q_table.get((state, action), 0.0)
        next_max = max(self.q_table.get((next_state, a), 0.0) for a in self.actions)
        new_q    = old_q + self.learning_rate * (reward + self.discount * next_max - old_q)
        self.q_table[(state, action)] = new_q

    def choose_action(self, state):
        if random.random() < self.epsilon:
            return random.choice(self.actions)
        q_vals = {a: self.q_table.get((state, a), 0.0) for a in self.actions}
        return max(q_vals, key=q_vals.get)

    def compute_reward(self):
        delta         = self.prev_stress - self.stress          # positive = stress reduced
        support_bonus = self.perceived_support * 0.02           # reward high support
        return -self.stress + 0.5 * delta + support_bonus

    # ---------- Action dynamics ----------
    def _execute_action(self, action):
        env_stress = self.model.daily_stress
        if action == "healthy_coping":
            self.stress            -= random.uniform(8, 15) + random.uniform(-3, 3)
            self.perceived_support += random.uniform(1, 3)
        elif action == "avoidance":
            self.stress -= random.uniform(4, 8)
            self.model.rebound_pool.append((self, random.uniform(2, 10)))
        elif action == "seek_support":
            if self.model.support_availability > 0.2:
                self.stress            -= random.uniform(10, 18)
                self.perceived_support += random.uniform(5, 10)
            else:
                self.stress += random.uniform(0, 6)
        self.stress            += env_stress * random.uniform(0.4, 1.0)
        self.stress             = max(0, min(self.stress, 100))
        self.perceived_support  = max(0, min(self.perceived_support, 100))

    # ---------- Social interaction ----------
    def social_interact(self):
        """
        Three agent-level social mechanisms:
        1. Stress contagion  – bidirectional: if a neighbour is more stressed,
           some of that stress rubs off (and vice-versa).
        2. Support sharing   – neighbours with higher perceived support lift yours.
        3. Peer Q-learning   – if a neighbour has higher cumulative reward, blend
           their Q-values into your own (observational learning).
        """
        if not self.neighbours:
            return

        # --- 1 & 2: stress contagion + support sharing ---
        avg_nbr_stress   = sum(n.stress            for n in self.neighbours) / len(self.neighbours)
        avg_nbr_support  = sum(n.perceived_support for n in self.neighbours) / len(self.neighbours)

        stress_delta   = (avg_nbr_stress  - self.stress)            # pos = neighbours more stressed
        support_delta  = (avg_nbr_support - self.perceived_support) # pos = neighbours more supported

        self.stress            += STRESS_CONTAGION * stress_delta
        self.perceived_support += SUPPORT_SHARING  * support_delta
        self.stress            = max(0, min(self.stress, 100))
        self.perceived_support = max(0, min(self.perceived_support, 100))

        # --- 3: peer Q-learning (observe the best neighbour) ---
        ql_neighbours = [n for n in self.neighbours if isinstance(n, QLearningCopingAgent)]
        if ql_neighbours:
            best_nbr = max(ql_neighbours, key=lambda n: n.cumulative_reward)
            if best_nbr.cumulative_reward > self.cumulative_reward:
                # Soft-update: blend best neighbour's Q-values into own table
                for key, nbr_q in best_nbr.q_table.items():
                    own_q = self.q_table.get(key, 0.0)
                    self.q_table[key] = own_q + PEER_LEARNING_RATE * (nbr_q - own_q)

    # ---------- Mesa step ----------
    def step(self):
        state  = self.get_state()
        action = self.choose_action(state)

        self.prev_stress = self.stress
        self._execute_action(action)
        self.social_interact()          # <-- agent-agent interaction

        reward     = self.compute_reward()
        next_state = self.get_state()

        self.cumulative_reward += reward
        self.memory.append((state, action, reward, next_state))
        self.update_q_value(state, action, reward, next_state)

        self.epsilon     = max(EPSILON_MIN, self.epsilon * EPSILON_DECAY)
        self.last_action = action

# 4. FIXED COPING AGENT  (with passive social interaction)
# ============================================================
class FixedCopingAgent(mesa.Agent):
    """
    Non-learning baseline. Still subject to stress contagion and support
    sharing from neighbours, but does NOT do peer Q-learning.
    """

    def __init__(self, model, strategy="mostly_avoidance"):
        super().__init__(model)
        self.stress            = random.uniform(30, 70)
        self.perceived_support = random.uniform(30, 70)
        self.strategy          = strategy
        self.agent_type        = f"Fixed-{strategy}"
        self.neighbours        = []
        self.cumulative_reward = 0.0

    def choose_action(self):
        probs_map = {
            "mostly_avoidance": [0.1, 0.7, 0.2],
            "mostly_healthy":   [0.7, 0.1, 0.2],
            "mostly_support":   [0.2, 0.2, 0.6],
        }
        probs = probs_map.get(self.strategy, [1/3, 1/3, 1/3])
        r = random.random()
        if r < probs[0]:             return "healthy_coping"
        elif r < probs[0]+probs[1]:  return "avoidance"
        else:                        return "seek_support"

    def _execute_action(self, action):
        env_stress = self.model.daily_stress
        if action == "healthy_coping":
            self.stress            -= random.uniform(5, 10)
            self.perceived_support += random.uniform(0, 2)
        elif action == "avoidance":
            self.stress -= random.uniform(3, 7)
            self.model.rebound_pool.append((self, random.uniform(1, 8)))
        elif action == "seek_support":
            if self.model.support_availability > 0.2:
                self.stress            -= random.uniform(8, 14)
                self.perceived_support += random.uniform(2, 6)
            else:
                self.stress += random.uniform(0, 4)
        self.stress            += env_stress * random.uniform(0.4, 1.0)
        self.stress             = max(0, min(self.stress, 100))
        self.perceived_support  = max(0, min(self.perceived_support, 100))

    def social_interact(self):
        """Stress contagion + support sharing only (no Q-learning)."""
        if not self.neighbours:
            return
        avg_nbr_stress  = sum(n.stress            for n in self.neighbours) / len(self.neighbours)
        avg_nbr_support = sum(n.perceived_support for n in self.neighbours) / len(self.neighbours)
        self.stress            += STRESS_CONTAGION * (avg_nbr_stress  - self.stress)
        self.perceived_support += SUPPORT_SHARING  * (avg_nbr_support - self.perceived_support)
        self.stress             = max(0, min(self.stress, 100))
        self.perceived_support  = max(0, min(self.perceived_support, 100))

    def step(self):
        action = self.choose_action()
        prev   = self.stress
        self._execute_action(action)
        self.social_interact()          # <-- agent-agent interaction
        reward = -(self.stress) + 0.5 * (prev - self.stress)
        self.cumulative_reward += reward

# 5. MODEL
# ============================================================
class MentalHealthModel(mesa.Model):
    """
    Environment + social network.
    After creating agents, build a random social network (Erdős–Rényi style)
    with each agent having exactly N_NEIGHBOURS connections.
    """

    def __init__(self, n_qlearners=10, n_fixed=10, seed=42):
        super().__init__(seed=seed)
        self.daily_stress         = 5.0
        self.support_availability = 0.6
        self.rebound_pool         = []

        for _ in range(n_qlearners):
            QLearningCopingAgent(self)

        strategies = ["mostly_avoidance", "mostly_healthy", "mostly_support"]
        for i in range(n_fixed):
            FixedCopingAgent(self, strategy=strategies[i % len(strategies)])

        # Build social network: each agent gets N_NEIGHBOURS random neighbours
        all_agents = list(self.agents)
        for agent in all_agents:
            candidates = [a for a in all_agents if a is not agent]
            agent.neighbours = random.sample(candidates, min(N_NEIGHBOURS, len(candidates)))

        self.datacollector = mesa.DataCollector(
            model_reporters={
                "Avg_Stress_QL": lambda m: round(
                    sum(a.stress for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))
                    / max(1, sum(1 for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))), 2),
                "Avg_Stress_Fixed": lambda m: round(
                    sum(a.stress for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, FixedCopingAgent))
                    / max(1, sum(1 for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, FixedCopingAgent))), 2),
                "Avg_Support_QL": lambda m: round(
                    sum(a.perceived_support for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))
                    / max(1, sum(1 for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))), 2),
                "Avg_Support_Fixed": lambda m: round(
                    sum(a.perceived_support for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, FixedCopingAgent))
                    / max(1, sum(1 for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, FixedCopingAgent))), 2),
                "Daily_Stress":   lambda m: round(m.daily_stress, 2),
                "Avg_Epsilon":    lambda m: round(
                    sum(a.epsilon for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))
                    / max(1, sum(1 for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))), 3),
                # NEW: social metrics
                "Avg_CumRew_QL": lambda m: round(
                    sum(a.cumulative_reward for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))
                    / max(1, sum(1 for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent))), 2),
                "Avg_CumRew_Fixed": lambda m: round(
                    sum(a.cumulative_reward for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, FixedCopingAgent))
                    / max(1, sum(1 for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, FixedCopingAgent))), 2),
                # Variance in stress (captures social clustering effects)
                "StressVar_QL": lambda m: round(
                    (lambda vals: sum((v - sum(vals)/len(vals))**2 for v in vals)/len(vals) if vals else 0)(
                        [a.stress for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, QLearningCopingAgent)]), 2),
                "StressVar_Fixed": lambda m: round(
                    (lambda vals: sum((v - sum(vals)/len(vals))**2 for v in vals)/len(vals) if vals else 0)(
                        [a.stress for a in m.agents if isinstance(a, FixedCopingAgent)]), 2),
            }
        )

    def _update_environment(self):
        mean_level = 5.0
        shock = random.gauss(0, 1.5)
        self.daily_stress += 0.3 * (mean_level - self.daily_stress) + shock
        self.daily_stress  = max(0, min(self.daily_stress, 12))

        # Support availability with seasonality
        t = getattr(self, "_step_count", 0)
        seasonality = 0.1 * math.sin(t / 30.0)
        self.support_availability += random.gauss(0, 0.05) + seasonality
        self.support_availability  = max(0.0, min(self.support_availability, 1.0))
        self._step_count = t + 1

        for agent, rebound in self.rebound_pool:
            agent.stress = max(0, min(agent.stress + rebound, 100))
        self.rebound_pool = []

    def step(self):
        self.agents.shuffle_do("step")
        self._update_environment()
        self.datacollector.collect(self)
